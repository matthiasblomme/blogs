Reference document for IBM TechXchange 2026 abstract submissions. Sourced from three places:

1. IBM Champions Enablement session "How to prepare your conference abstract and what reviewers look for" (April 8, 2026).

## 2. Form fields you have to fill in

All required.

- **Session Type** — dropdown. Two choices:
  - Technology Breakout (45 minutes): in-depth, technical session delivered by a Client, Partner or IBM expert, that demonstrates or explores IBM products, architectures, real-world case studies, and best practices.
  - Tech Talk (20 minutes): fast-paced, focused discussion featuring use cases, trends, and special interest topics shared by IBM technologists, advocates and community experts.
- **Software/infrastructure products** — multi-select. "Not on the list" available for Champions.
- **Why should this idea be considered for IBM TechXchange 2026?** — 800 char free text (the justification).
- **Technical Level** — dropdown with info icon. Pick one.
- **Industry** — dropdown. Pick one (or Cross-industry).
- **Session Title** — 100 char text field.
- **Session Abstract** — 800 char text field.
- **Agree to Terms** — checkbox.

If accepted, title and abstract may be edited by IBM. Speaker name, title, company, session title and abstract may be promoted on the conference website.

## 3. Topic angle Karen and Ben asked for

From Karen's email:

> "How about talking about something very much ACE field related ... like describing how ACE is actually deployed for real (eg deployment best practices or tuning best practices) ... or examples of successful modernization projects moving to containers? This kind of thing is harder for IBM development to blog about or present ... and it would be great to have some real-life success stories to point people at in the community ... he could do a whole series of them on different customer deployments/topics."

What that means for the abstract:

- Field perspective. Not product demo. Not "how ACE works".
- Real deployments, real engineering decisions, lived experience.
- The angle IBM development cannot publish themselves — including the honest "we picked X because Y, and here's where it bit us".
- A series is possible. Each abstract can stand alone but the body of work points at more.

## 4. What reviewers look for (positive signals)

Direct from Melissa Gurney Greene, Angie Borman, and Graeme Noseworthy in the Champions Enablement session.

- "How exciting is it as a technical person to attend it?" (Melissa's gut-check.)
- Use cases that help the most people. Production experience, lessons learned, specific keywords.
- Education, not entertainment, not "sharing". Moves an attendee from 101 to 102, or 102 to beyond.
- The "so what" — does it make someone in the audience better at their job?
- The "now what" — what is the concrete takeaway they walk out with?
- Conference promise: "You will leave a better, more qualified professional than the day you show up." Frame the abstract through that lens.
- Originality and personal voice. Passion comes through. "You be you."

## 5. What gets declined (negative signals)

Same source. These were named explicitly.

- Buzzword bingo:
  - "transformational synergy"
  - "digital transformation"
  - Any high-level marketing word without specifics.
- Sales or marketing pitch. This is a learning conference. IBM applies the same rule to its own staff.
- Vague verbs. "We transformed our deployment" tells a reviewer nothing. Replace with concrete numbers: "We took our production deployment cycle from 9 months to 9 minutes by..."
- Too much too soon. Trying to fit everything you know into 45 minutes. Pick the key points and trust people to come ask you for the rest.
- Middle-of-the-road talks. Not clearly aimed at any specific persona. Falls between two stools.
- AI-generated verbatim. They run analysis to flag likely AI-written submissions. Not an auto-reject, but a negative indicator. Using AI as a brainstorming partner is fine; copy-pasting AI output is not.
- Listing features without explaining why they matter.

## 6. Submission rules and logistics

- **One speaker preferred.** Co-presenter only with written justification on the form. Change from prior years.
- **Multi-part talks**: submit each part as a separate abstract. Label "Part 1 of 2" / "Part 2 of 2" in the abstract and explain in the justification. Email `speaker help` so they keep them connected during review.
- **"Not on the list" product option** is available for Champions if the product dropdown does not include what you need. Reference the product by name in the abstract instead.
- **Coaching** pre-conference, planned across July-September. On-site speaker coaches available for last-minute prep.
- **If accepted**: tell the world early and often. Set milestones (outline, draft, practice). Practice standing up in an empty room. Record yourself once.
- **If declined**: ask for feedback. The content is not wasted — recycle to user groups, online community, blog, video. Competition is high in some areas (Terraform was named as historically the most competitive).

## 7. What the abstract itself should do

Synthesised from sections 4 and 5 above, plus general guidance from the session.

- **Lead with the field**, not the product.
- **State the "from / to" in numbers, not adjectives.** Pull a measurable before/after from a real deployment.
- **Promise three specific takeaways** the audience walks out with. The "now what" reviewers explicitly want.
- **Pick the right track up front.** Don't try to be track-agnostic.
- **First person, lived experience.** "I ran into...", "what we shipped", "what broke at 3am".
- **Anonymise customer references.** "A customer we worked with", "customer 1 / 2 / 3". Never name a customer in the abstract or anywhere it could be promoted.
- **Avoid em dashes, marketing language, and setup-deflate punchlines.** Plain over polished. Dry over witty.

## 8. Quick checklist before you submit

- [ ] Title under 100 characters.
- [ ] Abstract under 800 characters.
- [ ] Justification under 800 characters.
- [ ] Three specific takeaways named in the abstract.
- [ ] No customer named anywhere. No internal paths. No internal naming conventions.
- [ ] No marketing language. No buzzword bingo. No "transformational" anything.
- [ ] At least one concrete number or "from / to" claim in the abstract or justification.
- [ ] Track and session type picked deliberately, not by default.
- [ ] Technical Level and Industry dropdowns chosen.
- [ ] Product field uses the right product, or "Not on the list" with the product named in the abstract.
- [ ] Read the abstract aloud once. If a sentence sounds written rather than said, rewrite it.
