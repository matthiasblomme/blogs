# Fine-tuning Granite Vision on Visdeurbel footage

*How we taught IBM Granite Vision to recognize fish in an underwater camera feed.*

---

This is part 3 of a series. Here is the short version of what came before.

The camera watches a riverbed 24/7. The goal is a Telegram notification when a fish swims past, without too many false alarms and without missing too many real ones.

**Part 1** was motion detection. OpenCV watches each frame, flags anything that moves, and saves a snapshot when the blob looks roughly fish-sized. Fast, lightweight, no AI. The problem: the river is murky. Sediment clouds, light shimmer, plant movement. All of it triggers the detector. The raw output was too noisy to send straight to Telegram.

**Part 2** added a vision model as a second opinion. Every motion hit goes to IBM Granite Vision with a detailed prompt: is there actually a fish here, or is this just sediment? Prompt tuning got the accuracy up to about 58% on the hard cases. Good enough to prove the concept. Not good enough to run unattended.

**Part 3**, this post, is where we stop arguing with the prompt and start training the model on the actual footage. Same camera, same murky water, labeled frames collected over several weeks. The goal: a model that knows what a fish looks like in *this specific stream*, not just fish in general.

---

## Where prompting stopped working

When you first connect a vision model to a real-world detection task, prompt engineering feels like magic. You write "Is there a fish in this image? Answer yes or no." and it works, sometimes. But as your use case gets more specific, prompting hits a ceiling.

The model was trained on the whole internet. It has a general idea of what a fish looks like, but it has never seen the murky, low-contrast footage of your specific riverbed camera. It doesn't know that the sediment clouds and water shimmer are *not* fish. It doesn't know the particular shapes and colors of the species that swim through your stream.

---

## How fine-tuning closes that gap

A large language model (or vision-language model) is pre-trained on massive, general datasets. Fine-tuning takes that pre-trained model and continues training it on a small, curated dataset specific to your task.

Think of it like this: the base model is a university graduate with broad knowledge. Fine-tuning is on-the-job training. You don't retrain them from scratch, you just teach them the specifics of your particular job.

In this case: show the model hundreds of labeled frames from the Visdeurbel stream, each tagged as "fish" or "no fish," and it learns the visual patterns that distinguish them in *this specific context*.

---

## Full fine-tuning wasn't an option

Granite Vision 3.2-2B has about 2 billion parameters. Training all of them would require:

- A multi-GPU setup (think: 4× A100s, ~$10/hour in the cloud)
- Days of training time
- Saving a 4+ GB checkpoint

For most practical fine-tuning tasks, you don't need this.

---

## Why LoRA

**LoRA** stands for *Low-Rank Adaptation*. Instead of updating all the weights in the model's attention layers, you inject small *adapter matrices* alongside them. These adapters are tiny, maybe 0.3% of the total parameters, but they're enough to shift the model's behavior significantly for your task.

Here's the math intuition: a weight matrix `W` (say, 4096×4096) has millions of parameters. LoRA approximates the *change* to that matrix as a product of two smaller matrices:

```
ΔW ≈ A × B
     (4096×16) × (16×4096)
```

Where 16 is the **rank** (`r`). You only train `A` and `B`. The original `W` stays frozen.

- Full fine-tune: ~400M parameters to train
- LoRA (rank=16): ~2M parameters to train

And crucially: the LoRA adapter is a separate file (~50–200 MB) that sits on top of the base model. You can share it, version it, and swap it in/out at runtime.

---

## Fitting it in VRAM

LoRA reduces the *trainable* parameters, but you still need to load the full model into GPU memory to do the forward pass. For a 2B model in 16-bit precision, that's ~4 GB of VRAM, which is tight on most consumer cards.

**QLoRA** adds one more trick: quantize the base model weights to **4-bit** before loading. The model weights are compressed from 16 bits to 4 bits per parameter using a format called NormalFloat4 (NF4), which is optimized for the distribution of neural network weights.

The result: the 2B model fits in ~3–4 GB of VRAM. Combined with LoRA's small adapter, you can fine-tune Granite Vision on a single consumer GPU with 12 GB VRAM (e.g., RTX 3060/3080/4070).

The training computation still happens in bfloat16 precision, only the stored weights are quantized.

## Our Dataset

I collected labeled frames directly from the Visdeurbel stream over several weeks. Two categories:

- **`images/fish/`** — frames where a fish is clearly visible (true positives from the existing detection pipeline, manually verified)
- **`images/no_fish/`** — frames that triggered the motion detector but contained no fish (false positives: sediment clouds, light changes, plant movement)

Images are 640×360 JPEG, the same resolution the stream is processed at.

I used an 80/20 train/validation split (see `prepare_dataset.py`), with random shuffling seeded for reproducibility.

Even 100–200 labeled images per class is enough to meaningfully shift model behavior. More is better, but the returns diminish past a few hundred.

## The Training Setup

### Model: `ibm-granite/granite-vision-3.2-2b`

IBM's Granite Vision is a compact (2B parameter) vision-language model built for instruction following. It's based on the LLaVA-Next architecture: a visual encoder (processes the image into tokens) feeds into a language model backbone (generates text). LoRA is applied only to the language backbone, the vision encoder stays frozen.

#### Why Granite Vision specifically?

A few reasons. It's built with edge deployment in mind: the 2B size is deliberate, not a limitation. The Q4_K_M quantized version is ~1.55 GB and runs on the same machine the stream monitoring already runs on, no dedicated GPU needed. We were already running it locally via llama.cpp in Docker, so a fine-tuned version stays in the same setup. It also benchmarks well for its size (DocVQA: 0.89, ChartQA: 0.87, TextVQA: 0.78), which for a binary yes/no task on consistent footage is more than enough. Apache 2.0 licensed too, so no usage restrictions, no API costs, and the labeled footage from a private stream stays private.

### Task framing

Each training sample is formatted as a chat conversation, using the same prompt that runs in production:

```
User: [image] You are reviewing a frame from a low-visibility underwater
      monitoring camera to decide whether a fish is visible.
      ...
      Reply with exactly one word: YES or NO
Assistant: YES
```

The prompt used during training must match the one used at inference time. If the model learns to respond to one question and is asked a different one in production, the fine-tuning doesn't transfer. In this project that means using the v8 prompt, the same one in `verifier.py` and `train.py`, consistently throughout.

### Key hyperparameters

| Parameter | Value | Why |
|-----------|-------|-----|
| `lora_rank` | 16 | Balances capacity vs. efficiency; 8 for very small datasets, 32 for larger |
| `lora_alpha` | 32 | Scaling factor = 2× rank is a common starting point |
| `lora_dropout` | 0.05 | Light regularization to prevent overfitting on small datasets |
| `learning_rate` | 2e-4 | Standard for LoRA; higher than full fine-tune because adapters train from random init |
| `epochs` | 3 | Enough for a small dataset; watch val loss to catch overfitting |
| `batch_size` | 1 + grad_accum=4 | Effective batch of 4; keeps VRAM usage low |
| `max_seq_len` | dynamic | SFTTrainer pads to the longest sample in each batch, no fixed limit needed |

## Results

After training on **101 fish / 303 not-fish** frames (324 train, 80 val), the fine-tuned adapter achieved:

| Metric | Score |
|--------|-------|
| **Accuracy** | 94.6% |
| **Precision** | 88.9% |
| **Recall** | 88.9% |
| **F1** | 88.9% |

Confusion matrix on the 80-sample validation set:

```
TP=16  FP=2  TN=54  FN=2
```

Only 4 mistakes out of 74 evaluated samples, 2 false positives (non-fish frames called fish) and 2 false negatives (fish frames missed). For a stream monitoring system where the LLM verifier is already one layer of filtering, this is a solid result with a relatively small dataset.

## Interpreting the Results

When evaluating fish detection, **recall matters more than precision**. Missing a fish (false negative) means a missed notification. Flagging a non-fish frame (false positive) just means a slightly annoying extra Telegram message that gets filtered by the LLM verifier downstream.

| Metric | What it means for us |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Accuracy** | Overall correct rate, good headline number |
| **Precision** | Of frames we called "fish", how many really were? |
| **Recall** | Of all fish frames, how many did we catch? |
| **F1** | Harmonic mean of precision and recall, use this to compare models |

With 200+ labeled fish samples, F1 > 0.90 should be achievable.

## Integrating the Adapter with the Production Pipeline

The fine-tuned LoRA adapter is a drop-in replacement for the base model in `verifier.py`. The only change needed is to load the adapter on top of the base model before serving:

```python
from peft import PeftModel

model = LlavaNextForConditionalGeneration.from_pretrained(BASE_MODEL_ID, ...)
model = PeftModel.from_pretrained(model, "model-tuning/output/granite-fish-lora")
```

Alternatively, serve it via llama.cpp (the same runtime used in the Docker setup) by merging the adapter and converting to GGUF. There are a few non-obvious steps here because `LlavaNextForConditionalGeneration` wraps the language model backbone inside a larger multimodal container that the GGUF converter doesn't understand.

The `export_gguf.py` script in `model-tuning/` handles this correctly. Run it from the `model-tuning/` folder:

```bash
python export_gguf.py --adapter granite-fish-lora-fast
```

It merges the LoRA weights, extracts the `GraniteForCausalLM` backbone (the part the converter actually needs), saves it, and prints the next commands to run.

Then install the converter dependencies and run the conversion:

```bash
git clone https://github.com/ggml-org/llama.cpp --depth 1
pip install gguf transformers sentencepiece protobuf
python llama.cpp/convert_hf_to_gguf.py output/granite-fish-lora-fast-merged --outtype q8_0 --outfile ../models/granite-vision.gguf
```

A few things worth noting:
- The repo moved from `ggerganov` to `ggml-org`. The old URL 404s.
- `q4_k_m` is not a valid `--outtype` for the converter directly. Use `q8_0` here, which is still ~2.5× smaller than fp16 and runs fine on a 16 GB GPU.
- The `requirements-convert_hf_to_gguf.txt` file pins `torch~=2.6.0`, which conflicts with a newer PyTorch install. Install only the packages you actually need: `gguf`, `transformers`, `sentencepiece`, `protobuf`.
- The mmproj (vision encoder) does not need to be converted or replaced — LoRA only fine-tunes the language backbone. The Docker build downloads it from HuggingFace as-is.

Once `models/granite-vision.gguf` is in place, rebuild the `llm` service and it will use the fine-tuned model automatically:

```bash
docker compose build llm
docker compose up
```

### Wiring it into Docker Compose

The production setup is two containers on the same Docker network. The `llm` service runs llama-server. The `visdeurbel` service runs the motion detection and calls the model over HTTP.

```yaml
services:
  llm:
    build:
      context: .
      dockerfile: Dockerfile.llm
    restart: unless-stopped
    deploy:
      resources:
        reservations:
          devices:
            - driver: nvidia
              count: 1
              capabilities: [gpu]
    healthcheck:
      test: ["CMD-SHELL", "wget -qO- http://localhost:8080/health | grep -q ok || exit 1"]
      interval: 10s
      timeout: 5s
      retries: 15
      start_period: 120s

  visdeurbel:
    build: .
    restart: unless-stopped
    depends_on:
      llm:
        condition: service_healthy
    env_file: .env
    environment:
      - LLM_URL=http://llm:8080
    volumes:
      - ./snapshots:/app/snapshots
```

The `deploy.resources.reservations` block passes the NVIDIA GPU into the container. Without this, llama-server falls back to CPU and each inference call takes 30-60 seconds instead of 1-2, well above the timeout. This requires the [NVIDIA Container Toolkit](https://docs.nvidia.com/datacenter/cloud-native/container-toolkit/install-guide.html) on the host.

The `healthcheck` solves a startup race condition. Loading a 2.5 GB model takes 60-90 seconds. Without a health check, `visdeurbel` would try to call the LLM endpoint while llama-server is still initialising, get a connection refused, and either crash or miss early detections. The `start_period: 120s` gives the model time to load before the health check starts counting failures.

`depends_on: condition: service_healthy` means `visdeurbel` won't start until llama-server responds with `{"status":"ok"}` on `/health`.

Credentials (Telegram bot token and chat ID) are read from a `.env` file via `env_file: .env`, so they stay out of source control.

Docker's internal DNS resolves `llm` to the right container. No exposed ports needed, the two services just talk to each other on the internal network.

### Calling the model from Python

llama-server exposes an OpenAI-compatible `/v1/chat/completions` endpoint. The verifier reads the snapshot, base64 encodes it, and sends it as a multimodal message:

```python
with open(snapshot_path, "rb") as f:
    b64 = base64.b64encode(f.read()).decode()

resp = requests.post(
    f"{llm_url}/v1/chat/completions",
    json={
        "model": "granite-vision",
        "messages": [{
            "role": "user",
            "content": [
                {"type": "image_url", "image_url": {"url": f"data:image/jpeg;base64,{b64}"}},
                {"type": "text", "text": "...prompt..."},
            ],
        }],
        "max_tokens": 10,
        "temperature": 0.0,
    },
    timeout=30,
)
answer = resp.json()["choices"][0]["message"]["content"].strip().lower()
confirmed = "yes" in answer
```

The image travels as a base64 data URL inside the JSON body. llama-server decodes it, runs the vision encoder, and returns the text response. The motion detector calls `verify(snapshot_path)`, gets back `True` or `False`, and decides whether to send the Telegram notification.

## If you can't run it locally

If the hardware is too weak or you just don't want your PC tied up for hours, a few platforms are worth knowing.

[Roboflow](https://roboflow.com) is the most complete option for managing and labeling your dataset. Upload images, annotate them in the browser, apply augmentations, and export to HuggingFace format. It also has a Train tab, though the resulting model is smaller and faster than a fine-tuned vision-language model and won't match it for nuanced low-visibility footage.

[HuggingFace AutoTrain](https://huggingface.co/autotrain) is the most natural fit if you're already using HuggingFace models. Upload two folders (`fish/` and `not-fish/`), pick a base model, configure epochs, and it runs on their infrastructure. The result lands directly in your Hub repository, ready to load with `transformers`.

[Google Colab](https://colab.research.google.com) gives you a free T4 GPU (16 GB VRAM). Mount your dataset from Drive and run `train_fast.py` exactly as-is. Training takes roughly the same 40 minutes. Good option if you want to keep the existing setup and just offload the compute.

[Label Studio](https://labelstud.io) is worth considering if the footage is private. It's open-source, runs in Docker, and nothing leaves your network.

```bash
docker run -p 8080:8080 heartexlabs/label-studio
```

For this project: Label Studio to label, Colab to train, download the adapter, deploy locally via Docker.

## What's Next

- **More data**, the single biggest lever. Every 2× increase in labeled samples improves accuracy.
- **Data augmentation**, horizontal flips, brightness jitter, and slight crops on the training images to improve robustness.
- **Active learning**, use the current model's uncertain predictions (near 50/50 confidence) to guide what to label next.
- **Rank tuning**, if overfitting is a problem, reduce `lora_rank` to 8. If the model plateaus early, try rank 32.
